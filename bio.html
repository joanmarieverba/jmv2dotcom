 1950s 
Born in 1953 in the Boston, Massachusetts area. My father was an active duty Air Force officer at the time.
The family moved to Minnesota in 1958, after Dad's enlistment was over. We started out in an apartment in Minneapolis. (I called the apartment building a "little castle." It was a brick building.)
Not long after that, we moved to Minnetonka, a western suburb of Minneapolis. I had started kindergarten in Minneapolis at age 4, and transferred to Clear Springs Elementary in Minnetonka after the move.
 1960s 
In the 1960s presidential election, I was definitely for JFK, who I had seen on TV (we watched the news during dinner every night). I decided then that I was going to be a Democrat.
Around 1962, my food allergies were finally diagnosed. I had avoided all the foods I was allergic to before being officially diagnosed, since they all smelled bad to me. My feeling is that I had food allergies almost from birth. (I had asthma and eczema as a baby.)
My second grade teacher put up a bulletin board showing the solar system, and I was immediately fascinated by astronomy. I checked out every book I could from the local library about astronomy and read it. My interest in science fiction grew from my interest in astronomy.
My maternal grandmother died in the early 1960s, from complications (stroke) from diabetes. My paternal grandmother died in 1965, from cancer.
Star Trek came along in 1966. I was an instant fan. (I also watched other 1960s shows, such as The Man From U.N.C.L.E. I belonged to the David McCallum Fan Club for many years.) In 1969, when I was in high school, I was introduced to fandom by Anthony Tollin, who I met at a high school speech festival.
 1970s 
When I was a senior in high school, my high school German class went to Europe. I graduated from high school in 1971.
I went to the University of Chicago in my freshman and sophomore years of college. While there, I traveled to my first convention, the Detroit Triple Fan Fair. I met Gene and Majel Roddenberry in an elevator (we were the only 3 there) and got their autographs.
I transferred to the University of Minnesota for my junior and senior college years, and graduated with a physics degree in 1975. I joined the Minnesota Science Fiction Society at that time, and became a regular contributor to MINNEAPA. I went to my first World Science Fiction Convention, Discon II, in 1974.
Around 1974, I became a member of the Star Trek Welcommittee. I joined The Mythopoeic Society in 1975. I also joined the Rivendell Discussion Group of the Mythopoeic Society.
After graduating from college, I went to the graduate school of astronomy at Indiana University, where I studied for 2 years. I became an Associate Instructor of Astronomy my second year there. However, I did not receive a graduate degree, and returned to Minnesota, where I took some graduate courses in computer programming at the University of Minnesota.
In 1978, I was hired as a FORTRAN progammer at Sperry/Unisys, writing software for airlines (flight schedules and hotel reservations). I moved into an apartment in 1979. Also in 1979, I drove to Winnipeg with other members of Minn-stf to see the solar eclipse there. Later that year, I joined Weight Watchers and lost the weight I had gained in graduate school. I also went to the World Science Fiction convention in England that year.
 1980s 
UNISYS sent me to Germany (Frankfurt) twice and Spain (Madrid) once, which were great experiences.
In 1982, I left UNISYS for Republic Airlines, a job that lasted less than six months. From there, I went to Control Data Corporation, and then UNISYS again, and then a tech startup which I left for health reasons in January 1988.
In 1984, my professional writing career started with the publication of my first professional short story and first magazine article.
In 1987, Star Trek: The Next Generation started. As with original Trek, I was an instant fan. I was a member of the Patrick Stewart Fan Club for many years.
In 1987, I had a health crisis, which caused me to have more food allergies. I lost my job, and had to move back with my parents.
In 1989, I got a job with Lerner Publications in Minneapolis, where I acquired a lot of publishing knowledge.
 1990s 
In 1991, Lerner Publications published my first book, Voyager: Exploring the Outer Planets. (It eventually went out of print, I got the rights back, and republished it in 2013.)
In 1993, Star Trek: Deep Space Nine premiered. I was an instant fan, especially of the Dr. Julian Bashir character. I was co-coordinator of the actor's fan club and published the club newsletter for many years.
In 1994, I founded my own press, FTL Publications.
In 1994, the Star Trek Welcommittee disbanded. I had been a member for over 20 years.
In 1995, I left Lerner Publications. I interned as an editorial assistant at Computer User magazine, then became a quality editor at TIPS/Bestmark.
In 1995, I was diagnosed with Carpal Tunnel Syndrome and wore wrist braces for about a year. After that I was able to avoid a recurrence with ergonomic keyboards.
In the early 1990s, I gathered my fanzines and began to write a Star Trek fan history. I had thought it would take me a summer, but it turned out to take me about 3 years. Boldly Writing was published in 1996, the first FTL Publications book.
In the late 1990s, I had another health crisis and added even more food allergies.
